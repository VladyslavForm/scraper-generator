Core Training: Beyond Traditional Ab Exercises

By Emily Parker, MSc, CSCS | Published: January 26, 2025

True core training extends far beyond crunches and sit-ups. Understanding the core's actual function revolutionizes how we approach midsection training and overall athletic performance.

What Is the Core?

The core includes all muscles between your shoulders and hips: rectus abdominis, obliques, transverse abdominis, erector spinae, glutes, and hip flexors. This muscular cylinder stabilizes the spine and transfers force between upper and lower body.

Core Function: Stability First

The primary role of core muscles is resisting movement and maintaining spinal alignment during dynamic activities. This anti-movement function is more important than the flexion and rotation emphasized in traditional ab work.

Training Principle

Exercises that challenge the core to resist extension, flexion, and rotation build functional strength that translates to sport and daily life.

Essential Core Training Categories

Anti-Extension

Exercises like planks and dead bugs teach the core to resist arching under load, protecting the lower back during overhead movements and running.

Anti-Rotation

Pallof presses and cable chops develop rotational stability, essential for athletic movement and spine protection during twisting motions.

Anti-Lateral Flexion

Side planks and suitcase carries build lateral stability, preventing energy leaks and maintaining proper alignment during unilateral movements.

Programming Core Work

Integrate core exercises at the beginning of workouts when fresh, or superset them with upper body exercises. Aim for quality over quantity, focusing on maintaining perfect position throughout each set.

Conclusion

Effective core training prioritizes stability and anti-movement over traditional flexion exercises. By training your core according to its actual function, you'll build practical strength that enhances performance and protects against injury.
