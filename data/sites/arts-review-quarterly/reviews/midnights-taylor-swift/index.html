Midnights: Taylor Swift's Nocturnal Confessions

By Alicia Thompson | Published: October 21, 2022
‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÖ 4/5

With Midnights, Taylor Swift returns to pop after her pandemic-era indie detours, bringing with her the introspection of folklore and evermore but wrapping it in shimmering synths and midnight-hour confessions. It's Swift at her most self-aware, exploring thirteen sleepless nights of anxiety, desire, self-loathing, and revenge fantasies.

The opening track "Lavender Haze" sets the tone‚Äîa hazy, dreamlike meditation on protecting love from outside scrutiny. Produced by Jack Antonoff, the song floats on waves of synths that recall 70s soft rock and modern dream pop. Swift's vocals are confident yet vulnerable, embodying the tension between public persona and private feeling.

"Swift has perfected the art of making personal anxiety feel universal‚Äîthese are her midnights, but they could be anyone's."

"Anti-Hero" is the album's undeniable centerpiece and its most brutally self-lacerating song. "It's me, hi, I'm the problem, it's me" became an instant cultural moment, but beneath the meme-able hook lies genuine pain‚ÄîSwift examining her own narcissism, self-sabotage, and fear of abandonment. It's pop music as therapy session, and it's brilliant.

The production throughout is immaculate. Antonoff and Swift have refined their collaborative chemistry to the point where their work feels seamless. "Maroon" uses red/wine imagery to explore the messy end of passion. "Snow on the Beach" (featuring Lana Del Rey, though far too subtly) captures the surreal feeling of unexpected connection. "Midnight Rain" employs pitch-shifted vocals to create emotional distance from a past self.

Not every song reaches these heights. "Vigilante Shit" aims for vengeful swagger but lands somewhat flat‚Äîthe production slick but the lyrics less sharp than Swift's best revenge songs. "Bejeweled" is fun but feels slight. The album occasionally prioritizes vibe over substance, which works better on some tracks than others.

"This is Swift as insomniac philosopher, turning sleeplessness into art and anxiety into anthem."

The 3 AM Edition bonus tracks complicate the album's narrative in interesting ways. "Would've, Could've, Should've" is devastating‚ÄîSwift directly addressing the harm of her relationship with John Mayer when she was nineteen. It's one of the rawest, angriest songs she's ever released, and its relegation to bonus track status feels like she couldn't quite bear to put it in the main sequence.

Lyrically, Swift remains unmatched in contemporary pop. "You're on your own, kid / You always have been" from "You're On Your Own, Kid" captures the loneliness of ambition with economical grace. "I'll stare directly at the sun but never in the mirror" from "Anti-Hero" is the kind of line that makes you stop and rewind. She's always been great, but she's somehow still getting better.

Midnights doesn't reinvent Swift's wheel‚Äîit's not as sonically adventurous as her pandemic albums or as culturally seismic as 1989. But it's a cohesive, thoughtful pop album from an artist at the height of her powers, using the midnight hour as a framework to explore her darkest thoughts and most vulnerable feelings. In an era of playlist singles, Swift still believes in albums that hang together, that reward close listening. That alone is worth celebrating. This is mature, self-aware pop music that respects its audience enough to be complex.
