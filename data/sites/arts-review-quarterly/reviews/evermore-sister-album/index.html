Evermore: Folklore's Worthy Sister

Five months after folklore's surprise release, Taylor Swift returned with evermore‚Äîa sister album that extends and deepens the aesthetic established by its predecessor. While some might view it as folklore outtakes, that assessment misses how evermore explores darker, more complex emotional territory. This is Swift leaning fully into her new creative freedom, pushing her storytelling even further.

Where folklore was introspective and wistful, evermore feels more outward-looking and narrative-driven. Swift, again collaborating with Aaron Dessner and Jack Antonoff, crafts murder ballads, historical fiction, and character studies that showcase her versatility as a songwriter. The production is slightly more adventurous‚Äîless hushed, more willing to experiment.

"Swift has found her creative sweet spot and refuses to leave‚Äîwhy would she when she's writing the best songs of her career?"

"willow" opens the album with graceful confidence, its witchy imagery and sinuous melody establishing that this isn't simply folklore part two. The song builds gradually, adding layers of instrumentation while maintaining the intimate vocal delivery. "champagne problems" tells a devastating breakup story from an unusual perspective‚Äîthe person doing the rejecting rather than being rejected.

The album's standout tracks rank among Swift's best work. "tolerate it" examines an unbalanced relationship with quiet devastation. "no body, no crime" feat. HAIM is a full country murder mystery that feels like a Chicks song reimagined. "coney island" featuring The National itself showcases Swift holding her own alongside Matt Berninger's distinctive baritone, two perspectives on faded glory and regret.

The long pond studio sessions documentary, released alongside the album, revealed Swift, Dessner, and Antonoff's creative process. Watching them workshop arrangements and discuss lyrics added depth to understanding these songs‚Äîthey emerged from genuine collaboration, not just Swift working with hired guns.

"These songs feel like autumn walks through memory and imagination‚Äîmelancholic but not hopeless, reflective without being static."

If there's a weakness, it's that evermore doesn't have quite the impact of discovery that folklore did. The aesthetic no longer surprises; some tracks feel like refinements rather than revelations. The album is also slightly less consistent, with a few songs that don't reach the heights of the best material.

But these are minor quibbles. evermore demonstrates that folklore wasn't a fluke or a pandemic quirk‚ÄîSwift has genuinely expanded her artistic range. She's comfortable in this indie-folk space, and her songwriting continues to sharpen. The character work, the narrative complexity, the willingness to write outside her own experience‚Äîall point to an artist hitting creative peak.

evermore cements 2020 as Swift's most artistically productive year. Two albums of uniformly high quality, both exploring new territory while maintaining her essential strengths as a melodist and lyricist. This is an artist unafraid to challenge herself and her audience, proving that commercial success and artistic growth aren't mutually exclusive. A worthy companion to folklore and another triumph in Swift's evolving discography.
