Cillian Murphy finally gets the lead role he deserves, and he's mesmerizing. The supporting ensemble is remarkable. Robert Downey Jr. delivers career-best work as Lewis Strauss, Oppenheimer's political nemesis, playing him with wounded pride and calculated vengeance. Emily Blunt's Kitty Oppenheimer cuts through every scene with sharp intelligence. Matt Damon, Florence Pugh, Josh Hartnett, and countless others make indelible impressions despite limited screen time.

Nolan's trademark non-linear structure serves the story well. The film intercuts between three timelines‚ÄîOppenheimer's rise, the Trinity test, and his 1954 security clearance hearing. What elevates Oppenheimer beyond technical excellence is its moral seriousness. Nolan doesn't provide easy answers about whether building the bomb was right or wrong. Instead, he shows us a man caught between scientific curiosity, patriotic duty, and human conscience. The film asks: what responsibility do scientists bear for how their discoveries are used? Can intellectual achievement justify mass destruction?

The final act, focused on Oppenheimer's persecution during the McCarthyism era, is deeply moving. Watching this titan reduced to answering petty accusations from small-minded bureaucrats is infuriating. This is essential cinema: a film about history that speaks urgently to our present moment, when scientific advancement continues to outpace our moral frameworks. Murphy and Nolan have created a haunting portrait of a man who gave us the power to end the world.
