Barbie: More Than a Cultural Phenomenon

By Jennifer Park | Published: July 21, 2023

Greta Gerwig's Barbie is a miraculous high-wire act‚Äîa film that satisfies Mattel's corporate interests while subverting them, that entertains masses while delivering sharp social commentary, that's both earnest and ironic. Against all odds, it works brilliantly as both candy-colored spectacle and thoughtful meditation on feminism, capitalism, and identity.

Margot Robbie is perfectly cast as Stereotypical Barbie, radiating the impossible perfection the doll represents while gradually revealing the existential anxiety beneath. When Barbie has her first thought about death, Robbie's face captures the horror of consciousness entering paradise. Ryan Gosling steals every scene as Ken, playing him as a himbo with surprising depth‚Äîhis musical number "I'm Just Ken" is an absurd, genuine highlight.

"Gerwig has created something genuinely new: a blockbuster that's critically engaged with its own existence as corporate product."

The production design by Sarah Greenwood is astonishing. Barbie Land feels handcrafted and artificial in exactly the right way‚Äîa plastic paradise that looks like a child's dollhouse brought to life. The costume design transitions from hyperreal pinks to muted reality with thematic purpose. Every frame is visually inventive while serving the story.

What makes Barbie remarkable is its willingness to engage with contradictions. It acknowledges Barbie's complicated legacy‚Äîsimultaneously empowering and limiting, a symbol of female achievement and impossible beauty standards. Gerwig doesn't resolve these tensions; she puts them onscreen and lets them collide. The film critiques patriarchy while acknowledging that men also suffer under rigid gender roles.

The "real world" sequences could have been the film's weak point, but Gerwig navigates them deftly. America Ferrera's monologue about the impossible standards women face is pointed and necessary. The Mattel executives, led by Will Ferrell, provide comic relief while representing corporate responses to feminist critique‚Äîperformative, self-serving, ultimately hollow.

"This is a film about becoming human‚Äîmessy, mortal, and meaningful in ways that perfection never can be."

The film stumbles slightly in its final act, where thematic ambition occasionally overwhelms narrative clarity. Some jokes don't land, and the ending's turn toward sincerity may feel abrupt. But these are minor issues in a film that successfully balances so many competing demands.

Mark Ronson and Andrew Wyatt's score, combined with a stellar soundtrack featuring Dua Lipa, Charli XCX, and others, gives the film infectious energy. The music underscores the film's tonal shifts‚Äîplayful, melancholic, triumphant‚Äîwithout overwhelming the dialogue and performances.

Barbie represents something rare in contemporary Hollywood: a major studio film with a clear authorial voice. Gerwig has somehow made a movie that works as pure entertainment while also functioning as commentary on entertainment itself. It's about the limits of empowerment feminism, the loneliness of perfection, and the beauty of embracing messiness and mortality. That it does all this while being genuinely funny and visually stunning is remarkable. This is smart, sophisticated filmmaking disguised as a toy commercial‚Äîand maybe the best argument yet for giving talented directors the resources to realize their visions.
