GUTS: Olivia Rodrigo's Sophomore Triumph

By Marcus Reynolds | Published: September 8, 2023
‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÖ¬Ω 4.5/5

The sophomore album curse is real in pop music‚Äîhow do you follow a record-breaking debut without repeating yourself or losing what made you special? Olivia Rodrigo answers with GUTS, an album that's angrier, funnier, and more confident than SOUR. She's avoided the slump by leaning into punk-pop aggression while maintaining her gift for devastating emotional honesty.

Where SOUR was heartbreak and betrayal, GUTS explores the messier territory of growing up in public, dealing with fame, and navigating relationships with more self-awareness. Rodrigo is 20 now, and that maturity shows‚Äînot in losing her edge, but in sharpening it, directing her anger more precisely while laughing at herself more freely.

"Rodrigo proves she's not a one-album wonder but a generational voice with staying power and artistic vision."

The album opens with explosive energy that immediately establishes this isn't SOUR part two. Producer Dan Nigro returns, and their creative partnership has deepened. The production is fuller, more adventurous, incorporating 90s alt-rock influences, riot grrrl punk, and contemporary pop with confident eclecticism.

The singles showcase Rodrigo's range. "vampire" became an instant classic, its dramatic piano opening giving way to a soaring chorus about emotional manipulation. "get him back!" is wickedly funny, playing with the double meaning of its title. "bad idea right?" captures the self-aware recklessness of young adulthood with infectious energy and knowing humor.

Lyrically, Rodrigo continues to excel at specificity. She writes lines that feel pulled from text messages and diary entries, making universal experiences feel intensely personal. Her willingness to be messy, petty, and contradictory makes her relatable‚Äîthese aren't polished, perfectly resolved emotions but raw, ongoing struggles.

"This is pop-punk for a generation raised on social media anxiety and existential dread, delivered with humor and heart."

The album's back half shows Rodrigo's growth as a songwriter. Ballads like "making the bed" and "the grudge" demonstrate emotional depth beyond teenage heartbreak. She's writing about self-sabotage, fame's costs, and complicated feelings that resist easy categorization. These songs hint at where Rodrigo might go next‚Äîeven more ambitious, even more complex.

If there's a criticism, it's that a few tracks feel like variations on established formulas rather than new territory. The album is shorter than SOUR but occasionally still feels padded. However, even lesser tracks maintain high energy and catchy melodies‚Äîthere are no true duds, just songs that don't reach the heights of the best material.

What makes GUTS impressive is how Rodrigo has evolved without losing what made her special. She's still writing confessional pop-rock, still channeling teenage emotion, but with more humor, more anger, more self-awareness. The angst isn't gone‚Äîit's matured, become more articulate, more willing to laugh at itself.

GUTS cements Olivia Rodrigo as one of pop's most exciting voices. She's not trying to reinvent herself or chase trends‚Äîshe's deepening her sound, sharpening her songwriting, and proving that her debut success wasn't luck but the announcement of genuine talent. This is a confident, engaging sophomore album that bodes well for a long, creative career. Rodrigo has guts, and she's got the goods to match.
