Folklore: Swift's Indie Pivot

By Alicia Thompson | Published: July 24, 2020
‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÖ 5/5

Released as a surprise during the pandemic's isolation, folklore marked Taylor Swift's sharpest artistic left turn‚Äîa collection of indie-folk story-songs that traded stadium anthems for intimate campfire confessionals. Collaborating with Aaron Dessner of The National and longtime partner Jack Antonoff, Swift created her most critically acclaimed album, proving her artistry extends far beyond the pop lane.

The album opens with "the 1," a wistful meditation on a past relationship that sets the tone‚Äîreflective, melancholic, conversational. Swift's vocals are softer here than on previous records, nearly whispered at times, drawing listeners into confidences rather than projecting to arena rafters. The restraint is powerful, showing maturity and confidence in her craft.

"Swift strips away the production gloss to reveal the storytelling prowess that's been there all along, now free to breathe."

Dessner's production aesthetic‚Äîacoustic guitars, gentle piano, subtle strings‚Äîprovides the perfect canvas for Swift's increasingly sophisticated songwriting. Tracks like "exile" (featuring Bon Iver's Justin Vernon) and "my tears ricochet" showcase her ability to write from multiple perspectives, creating narrative tension and emotional complexity.

The album's fictional narratives represent new territory for Swift. The "teenage love triangle" songs chart relationships between fictional characters across multiple tracks, showing Swift's growth as a storyteller. She's no longer just chronicling her own life; she's building worlds, inhabiting characters, writing from viewpoints far from her own experience.

"cardigan," the album's lead single, deserved its commercial success. Its bridge exemplifies Swift's strength‚Äîascending melody, clever wordplay, emotional escalation that feels both earned and inevitable. The production builds subtly, strings swelling just enough to lift the emotion without overwhelming the intimacy.

"This is Swift untethered from commercial expectation‚Äîwriting for the love of writing, and it shows in every line."

Lyrically, folklore showcases Swift at her sharpest. The imagery is vivid and specific‚Äî"I can see us twisted in bedsheets," "your braids like a pattern," "your midas touch on the chevy door." She's always been a detail-oriented writer, but here those details accumulate into fully realized scenes and characters.

Not every track reaches the same heights. The album's back half occasionally meanders, and at 16 tracks, some editing might have sharpened the impact. But even lesser tracks maintain the album's atmospheric coherence, contributing to its overall sense of mood and place.

What makes folklore remarkable is how fully Swift commits to this aesthetic shift. There's no hedging, no "still doing pop" safety nets. She trusts her collaborators, trusts the material, and trusts her audience to follow her into quieter, stranger territory. That trust pays off spectacularly.

folklore proved Swift's artistic versatility and silenced critics who'd pigeonholed her as purely a pop star. It's an album that rewards close, repeated listening‚Äîa pandemic companion that felt perfectly suited to isolation's introspection. This is Swift as album artist, prioritizing cohesion and craft over singles and streaming numbers. A career-defining masterpiece that expanded what Taylor Swift music could be.
