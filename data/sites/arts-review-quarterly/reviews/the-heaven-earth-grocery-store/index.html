The Heaven & Earth Grocery Store

By Robert Williams | Published: August 8, 2023

James McBride has built his literary reputation on excavating forgotten corners of American history and breathing vibrant life into them. With The Heaven & Earth Grocery Store, he returns to his greatest strengths: rich ensemble storytelling, moral complexity, and the examination of how communities forge bonds across seemingly insurmountable divides. This novel is sprawling, generous, and deeply humane.

Set in the Chicken Hill neighborhood of Pottstown, Pennsylvania, during the 1920s through 1970s, the novel opens with a mystery‚Äîa skeleton discovered in a well‚Äîand then spirals backward and outward to tell the interconnected stories of the Black and Jewish families who share this marginalized community. At the heart of it all is the Heaven & Earth Grocery Store, run by the Jewish shopkeeper Moshe and his African American wife Chona.

"McBride reminds us that the bonds of community are forged not in grand gestures but in daily acts of seeing and honoring each other's humanity."

McBride's greatest gift is his ability to create characters who feel lived-in and authentic. Chona, disabled by polio but possessing an indomitable spirit, emerges as the novel's moral center. Her determination to protect Dodo, a deaf Black boy threatened by institutionalization, drives much of the plot. But McBride never reduces his characters to their struggles; they're fully realized people with desires, flaws, humor, and dignity.

The novel's structure is deliberately loose, even digressive. McBride takes his time, allowing characters to wander into view and tell their stories at their own pace. Some readers may find this meandering, but it's essential to the novel's project. McBride is showing us how community actually works‚Äînot through clean narrative arcs, but through the messy, overlapping lives of people who depend on each other for survival.

The historical detail is vivid without being overwhelming. McBride captures the texture of life in mid-century America: the jazz clubs and illegal liquor, the casual cruelties of segregation and anti-Semitism, the ways marginalized communities created their own institutions when mainstream society rejected them. The Heaven & Earth Grocery Store becomes a symbol of this resilience, a place where people gather not just to shop but to maintain their humanity in an often inhumane world.

"In McBride's hands, history never feels distant. It pulses with the urgency of the present, reminding us that the questions of belonging, justice, and solidarity remain unresolved."

The novel tackles difficult themes‚Äîableism, particularly regarding the horrific treatment of disabled people in state institutions; racism and anti-Semitism; class exploitation. But McBride balances these heavy subjects with moments of joy, humor, and genuine warmth. His characters face enormous challenges, but they also dance, laugh, scheme, and love. This tonal balance keeps the novel from becoming relentlessly grim while never diminishing the real dangers his characters face.

If there's a weakness, it's that the novel's expansive approach sometimes sacrifices narrative momentum. The mystery that opens the book recedes for long stretches, and some subplots feel more essential than others. McBride seems more interested in capturing a world than in maintaining suspense, which is a valid artistic choice but may frustrate readers expecting a tighter plot.

The novel's resolution, when it comes, is both heartbreaking and affirming. McBride shows us that protecting one vulnerable child required the coordination of an entire community, people putting themselves at risk because it was the right thing to do. In our current moment of fractured communities and eroded social trust, this vision of collective care feels both nostalgic and aspirational.

The Heaven & Earth Grocery Store is McBride working in his signature mode: big-hearted, historically grounded storytelling that refuses to flatten the complexity of American life. It's a novel about how the most marginalized people often best understand the necessity of solidarity, and how small acts of decency accumulate into something that might be called grace. This is a generous, deeply felt novel that reminds us of fiction's power to honor forgotten lives and reveal the dignity in everyday resistance.
