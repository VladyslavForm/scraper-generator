Tom Lake by Ann Patchett

By Robert Williams | Published: August 1, 2024

Ann Patchett's Tom Lake is a luminous family drama about memory, theater, and the stories we tell ourselves about our lives. Set during the early COVID pandemic on a Michigan cherry orchard, it follows Lara as she recounts to her three adult daughters the story of her youthful acting career and romance with a now-famous actor. What emerges is Patchett's warmest, most emotionally generous novel‚Äîa meditation on how we construct narrative from the chaos of lived experience.

The structure is elegant: present-day scenes of the family working the orchard alternate with Lara's memories of her time at Tom Lake, a summer stock theater where she performed in Our Town and fell in love. Patchett uses Thornton Wilder's play as both framework and mirror, its themes of appreciation, mortality, and the preciousness of ordinary life resonating throughout her novel.

"Patchett has crafted a love letter to ordinary life‚Äîits rhythms, its satisfactions, and the ways we make meaning from memory."

Lara is a complex narrator. She's telling her daughters about first love and lost possibilities, but she's also revealing how she chose this life‚Äîmarriage to a good man, raising children on an orchard‚Äîover the uncertainty of an acting career and a volatile relationship with a man who became famous. The novel asks: how do we reconcile the lives we imagined with the lives we built?

The three daughters are distinct personalities, each with their own relationship to their mother's story. Emily, the eldest and most practical; Maisie, the middle child searching for direction; and Nell, the youngest and most artistic. Their dynamics feel authentic‚Äîthe teasing, the competition, the deep love beneath surface tensions. Patchett excels at rendering family relationships in all their complicated affection.

Patchett's prose is characteristically graceful. She writes with such apparent ease that you might miss how precisely each sentence is crafted. Her descriptions of the orchard, the theater, and domestic life create vivid sensory experiences without overwrought detail. The writing serves the story rather than calling attention to itself‚Äîa mark of mature craft.

"This is a novel about choosing contentment over drama, and finding that contentment contains its own forms of meaning and beauty."

The pandemic setting adds poignancy without overwhelming the narrative. The family's isolation creates space for storytelling, for being together in ways their busy adult lives typically don't allow. There's something healing about the novel's vision of family weathering crisis through work, conversation, and presence‚Äîa gentle antidote to pandemic anxiety.

If there's a criticism, it's that the novel is almost too gentle. Conflicts arise and resolve with minimal lasting damage. The famous actor ex-boyfriend, who could have been a complicating presence, remains mostly offstage. Some readers may crave more dramatic tension or higher stakes. But this restraint feels intentional‚ÄîPatchett is interested in quiet satisfactions rather than melodrama.

The novel's engagement with Our Town enriches both works. Patchett draws parallels between Wilder's celebration of everyday life and her own narrative without being heavy-handed. Both works ask us to notice and appreciate the ordinary moments that constitute most of our lives‚Äîthe conversations, the shared work, the accumulation of days into a life.

Tom Lake is Ann Patchett at her most assured and humane. She's written a novel that champions the choices we make, the lives we build, and the stories we shape from memory. It's warm without being sentimental, wise without being preachy, and deeply satisfying in its affirmation that ordinary life‚Äîfamily, work, love‚Äîcan be enough. This is mature, compassionate fiction that reminds us why we read: to see our own lives reflected back with clarity, beauty, and understanding.
