Lisbon's Azulejos: Tiles That Tell Stories

By Miguel Santos

Published: September 28, 2025

Portugal

Portugal's iconic blue and white ceramic tiles—azulejos—are more than decoration. They're art, history, and cultural identity glazed onto surfaces across Lisbon.

Origins and Evolution

Azulejos arrived in Portugal via Moorish influence in the 15th century. I visited the National Tile Museum to understand their evolution from geometric Islamic patterns to pictorial narratives depicting Portuguese history, religion, and daily life.

Finding Azulejos Everywhere

Lisbon's azulejos appear on everything: church interiors, building facades, train stations, and metro stations. The São Bento train station features 20,000 tiles depicting Portuguese history. Modern metro stations commission contemporary artists to create new azulejo art.

Traditional Workshops

I visited a traditional azulejo workshop where artisans still hand-paint tiles using centuries-old techniques. The process requires multiple firings and precise painting—a single large panel can take months to complete.

Tile Spotting

Look up! Many beautiful azulejo panels are on upper floors where tourists rarely glance. The Alfama district and churches hide the finest examples.

Azulejos turn Lisbon into an open-air gallery where art and function merge. They're quintessentially Portuguese—beautiful, practical, and everywhere.
