Chasing Northern Lights in Iceland

By Erik Johansson

Published: October 22, 2025

Iceland

The aurora borealis is one of nature's most spectacular phenomena. After three trips to Iceland, I finally witnessed the northern lights dancing across the Arctic sky in a display that exceeded my wildest expectations.

Understanding the Aurora

The northern lights occur when charged particles from the sun collide with gases in Earth's atmosphere. But understanding the science doesn't diminish the magic. I spent hours researching KP indices, cloud cover, and aurora forecasts, learning that patience and flexibility are as important as luck.

My Successful Hunt

On my fourth night in Iceland, the aurora forecast showed strong activity. We drove an hour from Reykjavik to escape light pollution, parking near Thingvellir National Park. At 11 PM, a faint green glow appeared on the horizon. Within minutes, it exploded into curtains of green, purple, and red light rippling across the entire sky.

The aurora moved like living fabric, swirling, pulsing, and dancing to its own rhythm. Photos can't capture the movement, the scale, or the feeling of standing beneath this cosmic light show. For 40 minutes, we watched in silence, occasionally gasping at particularly bright displays.

Aurora Hunting Tips

Visit between September and April. Check aurora forecasts daily. Drive away from cities. Bring warm clothes—you'll be outside for hours. Use a tripod for photos. Most importantly, put the camera down sometimes and just watch.

Seeing the northern lights was a bucket-list moment that lived up to every expectation. Iceland's dark winter skies offer one of Earth's greatest natural spectacles—and the hunt itself becomes part of the adventure.
